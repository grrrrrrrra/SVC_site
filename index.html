Abstract

Natural language instructions in human‚Äìrobot interaction often contain subtle ambiguities that hinder reliable interpretation. These arise when a single instruction can be understood in multiple ways, assigning conflicting semantic roles to objects, tools, or participants. To address this, we propose Semantic Valency Conflict (SVC) ‚Äî a cognitively inspired, logit-free method for detecting ambiguity in robot-directed instructions. SVC identifies divergences in role assignments across alternative interpretations of a predicate using large language models to infer context-sensitive semantic frames.

The method is model-agnostic and compatible with both open- and closed-source LLMs. It produces interpretable outputs that highlight which parts of the instruction are ambiguous and what predicate‚Äìargument conflicts cause them. Evaluated on AmbiK and IntroPlan Mobile Manipulation datasets, SVC outperforms existing baselines in detecting fine-grained ambiguities in natural language robot instructions.

Method

We define Semantic Valency Conflict as a condition where multiple interpretations of a lexeme activate incompatible semantic valency profiles in a given environment. Each predicate triggers one or more cognitive frames, each with its own set of expected semantic roles (valency profile). Ambiguity occurs when these profiles cannot be grounded to overlapping entities in context.

The SVC architecture mirrors cognitive mechanisms of frame activation and conflict resolution, consisting of four stages:

  • Predicate Identification ‚Äî Extracts lexical units that serve as predicates.
  • Frame Induction ‚Äî Uses LLMs to dynamically infer possible semantic frames.
  • Role Alignment ‚Äî Aligns predicted roles and actants with contextual entities.
  • Conflict Evaluation ‚Äî Detects incompatible valency profiles indicating ambiguity.

Unlike heuristic or logit-based methods, SVC operates without access to logits or static knowledge bases, making it both interpretable and adaptable.

Results

SVC was evaluated on two benchmark datasets ‚Äî AmbiK and IntroPlan Mobile Manipulation ‚Äî across three LLMs: GPT-3.5, Mistral-7B, and Qwen1.5-7B. Metrics include Help Rate (HR), Correct Help Rate (CHR), and Intent Alignment (IA).

Compared to baselines such as KnowNo, LAP, and LoFreeCP, SVC achieved the highest CHR (up to 0.77) and CHQ (Contextual Help Quality) across most instruction types, particularly in user preference and common-sense ambiguity cases. For example, in the instruction ‚ÄúPour me some tea‚Äù with both green and black tea available, SVC correctly identifies a valency conflict.

The method generalizes across models, achieving the most stable performance on Mistral-7B due to its deterministic completions. SVC effectively balances ambiguity sensitivity with intent alignment, avoiding over-triggering seen in baseline methods.

Conclusion

We introduced Semantic Valency Conflict (SVC) ‚Äî a cognitively motivated, logit-free framework for detecting ambiguity in robot-directed language instructions. SVC analyzes semantic role conflicts between cognitive frames, offering fine-grained and interpretable ambiguity detection suitable for both open- and closed-source models.

Future work will focus on extending SVC to multimodal settings and integrating disambiguation strategies for real-time robotic dialogue systems.
